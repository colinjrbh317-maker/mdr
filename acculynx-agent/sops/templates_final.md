## Layer 9: Drip (Post-Close Value Ladder)
**Goal:** Turn a one-time customer into someone who calls MDR first for every exterior project and sends their neighbors over. Deliver real value at every step before asking for anything.
**Tone:** Warm and natural. Each touch sounds like a trusted contractor staying in touch, not a campaign.
**Ladder logic:**
1. T1 (Day 30): No offer. Genuine check-in. Ask if everything is looking and performing the way it should. Trust-building only.
2. T2 (Day 90): Real free service. Complimentary exterior tune-up ($150 value, on MDR). Low-friction: MDR does not need them home.
3. T3 (Day 180): $250 referral. A check mailed to their door per signed referral. No cap.
4. T4 (Day 365): Free annual inspection plus 10% off any add-on work booked at the same visit.

Safety gate: before drafting any drip message, confirm job invoiced and paid in full, zero open punch-list items, and no unresolved disputes or active change orders. Any condition fails = ESCALATION_REQUIRED.

Each touch must read as a continuation of the relationship, not a campaign. Reference the install in the opener, then deliver the touch's specific offer.

### Touch 1, Text, Day 30 post-close - Soft check-in (no offer)
**Goal:** Re-open the relationship as a trusted contractor who genuinely cares how the work held up. No offer, no ask for referrals, no mention of other products. ONE ask: "has anything caught your eye since the install?"
**Tone:** Warm and natural. Like a contractor who takes pride in their work and wants to know how it performed.
**Length target:** 30 to 65 words. Tight 3-line signature.
**Voice:** First person, warm, casual. No offers yet. Not asking for anything at Day 30 is a deliberate trust move.

## Compilation Notes

### Win Count by Copywriter

| Version | Style | Wins |
|---------|-------|------|
| v5 | Conversational | 23 |
| v1 | Direct Response | 5 |
| v4 | Urgency + Social Proof | 1 |
| v2 | Relationship | 1 |
| v3 | Educator | 0 |

**v5 (Conversational) dominated** with 23 of 30 touchpoint wins. This is not surprising given the selection criteria heavily weighted "sounds like a real person texted this from their phone." v5 was written specifically to match real MDR rep voice samples, and it shows.

### Hardest Layers to Pick (Closest Competition)

1. **Layer 4, Touch 3 (Day 10 Email):** v1 vs. v5 was the tightest call. v5's conversational tone nearly won, but v1's bolded section headers (Financing / Warranty / Scheduling) made it more scannable for what is the single most important email in the entire pipeline. Scannability won.

2. **Layer 5, Touch 3 (Day 38 Email):** v4 vs. v5 was close. v5 had a cleaner voice, but v4's approach of quoting review themes rather than fabricating specific reviews was more credible and more compelling. v4 won on substance.

3. **Layer 6, Touch 1 (Day 52 Email):** v5 vs. v3 was competitive. v3's technical details (ice and water shield, drip edge, ventilation ratio) would impress a knowledgeable homeowner, but v5's "we don't use day-laborers" bullet hits harder for a broader audience and the overall tone was more appropriate for a going-cold email.

4. **Layer 10, Text vs. Email:** v1 and v2 split the channel wins here. v1's text was warmer; v2's email had the best single line ("this is your home and your decision").

### Recommended Hybrids

1. **Layer 3, Touch 1 (Post-Inspection Email):** Consider combining v5's conversational tone with v3's educational content about what makes estimates different (shingle grade, underlayment, decking). v5 wins for first deployment, but v3's educational angle could be A/B tested as a variant.

The final template set has strong voice consistency because v5 won the vast majority of touches. The one area to watch: v1 and v2 use "Hi [Name]" while v5 uses "Hey [Name]." The final set mixes both, which is acceptable, "Hi" reads naturally in emails where formality is slightly higher, while "Hey" works in texts and conversational emails. No reader would notice the inconsistency across separate messages days or weeks apart.
