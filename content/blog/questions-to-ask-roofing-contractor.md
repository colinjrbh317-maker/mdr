A new roof is one of the biggest investments you'll make as a homeowner. Whether you're dealing with storm damage, aging shingles, or a persistent leak, picking the right contractor makes the difference between a roof that protects your family for decades and one that gives you problems within a year.

The tricky part? Every contractor who knocks on your door or shows up in a Google search will tell you they're the best. So how do you actually separate the professionals from the fly-by-night operators?

Start by asking these 10 questions. The answers will tell you everything you need to know.

---

### 1. Are You Licensed and Insured in Virginia?

This is the bare minimum. In Virginia, roofing contractors need a Class A, B, or C contractor's license depending on the project size. Ask to see the license and verify it with the Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR). You also want proof of both general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. If a worker gets injured on your property and the contractor doesn't carry workers' comp, you could be on the hook. Any contractor who dodges this question or says their paperwork is "at the office" should be crossed off your list immediately.

**Red flag:** A contractor who can't produce license and insurance documentation on the spot — or who pressures you to skip this step.

### 2. How Long Have You Been in Business Locally?

There's a reason this matters. After every major storm, crews from out of state flood into areas like the New River Valley and Roanoke to chase insurance claims. They do quick, cheap work, collect the check, and disappear. When something goes wrong six months later, they're unreachable. Look for a contractor with an established local presence — a physical address, local references, and a track record in your area. A company that's been working in Southwest Virginia for years has a reputation to protect, which means they're more likely to stand behind their work.

**Good answer:** A specific number of years, a local address you can verify, and a willingness to connect you with past customers in your area.

### 3. What Manufacturer Certifications Do You Hold?

Not all roofing contractors are created equal in the eyes of shingle manufacturers. Certifications like GAF Master Elite, CertainTeed SELECT ShingleMaster, or Owens Corning Platinum Preferred mean the contractor has met strict standards for training, insurance, and installation quality. More importantly, these certifications unlock better warranty options for you. A GAF Master Elite contractor, for example, can offer the GAF Golden Pledge warranty — 50 years of material coverage plus 25 years of workmanship coverage. A non-certified installer using the same shingles can only offer the basic manufacturer warranty with zero workmanship protection.

**Good answer:** Specific certification names with details on what enhanced warranties they can offer you.

### 4. Can You Provide Local References and Photos of Recent Work?

Any established contractor should be able to hand you a list of homeowners they've worked for recently — not just names from five years ago. Ask for three to five references from the past 12 months, and actually call them. Ask those homeowners if the crew showed up on time, kept the job site clean, and handled any issues that came up. Also ask to see before-and-after photos of completed projects, especially jobs similar to yours. A contractor who's proud of their work will have plenty to show you.

**Red flag:** Vague answers like "we've done hundreds of roofs" without being able to connect you with a single specific homeowner.

### 5. Will You Provide a Detailed Written Estimate?

A handwritten number on the back of a business card is not an estimate. You want a written proposal that breaks down materials (brand, product line, color), labor, tear-off and disposal costs, timeline, payment schedule, and warranty details. This document protects both of you. It also makes it much easier to compare bids apples-to-apples when you're getting multiple quotes. If one contractor's bid is significantly lower than the others, that detailed breakdown will help you figure out where they're cutting corners — cheaper materials, skipping underlayment, or not replacing damaged decking.

**Good answer:** A multi-page proposal with line items, material specs, and clear payment terms — delivered before you're asked to sign anything.

### 6. What Does Your Warranty Cover?

This is where a lot of homeowners get burned. There are two warranties that matter: the manufacturer's material warranty and the contractor's workmanship warranty. The material warranty covers defects in the shingles or other products. The workmanship warranty covers the installation itself — and this is where most problems actually happen. Ask how long their workmanship warranty lasts and what it covers. Get it in writing. A contractor who offers a 2-year workmanship warranty is telling you something very different than one who offers 10 or 25 years.

**Red flag:** A contractor who talks only about the "lifetime warranty" on the shingles without mentioning workmanship coverage at all.

### 7. Who Will Actually Be on My Roof?

Some contractors use their own trained crews. Others subcontract the labor to whoever is available that week. Neither approach is automatically wrong, but you deserve to know. Ask whether the crew members are employees or subcontractors, whether they're covered by insurance, and who will be supervising the job on-site. You want to know that the person installing your roof has been trained and that someone is checking the work as it happens — not just showing up at the end to collect the final payment.

**Good answer:** "We use our own crews. Your project manager will be on-site daily, and here's their contact information."

### 8. How Do You Handle Unexpected Damage or Changes?

Once the old shingles come off, there's always a chance the crew will find rotted decking, damaged flashing, or other issues that weren't visible during the initial inspection. A professional contractor will have a clear process for this: they'll stop work, document the damage with photos, explain what needs to be fixed, and give you a price before proceeding. This should be spelled out in your contract. You should never be surprised by a bill that's thousands more than the original quote with no explanation.

**Red flag:** A contractor who won't discuss change orders or says "we'll figure it out as we go."

### 9. What's the Timeline, and How Do You Handle Weather Delays?

In the Christiansburg and greater Roanoke area, weather can change plans fast — especially in spring and fall. Ask for a realistic start date and expected completion time. A typical residential re-roof takes one to three days depending on the size and complexity. Also ask how they handle rain delays and what they do to protect your home if work can't be completed in a single day. Tarping and securing the roof overnight should be standard practice, not an afterthought.

**Good answer:** A specific start date within a reasonable window, a clear plan for weather delays, and a commitment to secure the roof at the end of each workday.

### 10. What's Your Payment Structure?

Be cautious of any contractor who demands full payment upfront. A standard arrangement is a deposit (typically 10-30%) to secure your spot on the schedule, with the balance due upon completion and your final inspection. Some contractors offer financing options, which can be a sign of an established business. Never pay cash without a receipt, and never make a final payment until you've walked the property and confirmed the work meets your expectations and matches the contract.

**Red flag:** Demanding 50% or more upfront, cash-only payments, or pressuring you to pay before the job starts.

## Bonus Red Flags to Watch For

Beyond the questions above, keep an eye out for these warning signs:

- **Door-to-door pressure after a storm.** Legitimate contractors don't need to chase you down. Storm chasers work your neighborhood hard, do fast work, and are gone before you notice problems.
- **No physical business address.** If the only contact info is a cell phone number, think twice.
- **Unwillingness to pull permits.** Your local building department requires permits for roof replacements. A contractor who wants to skip permits is cutting corners you can't afford.
- **A quote that's dramatically lower than everyone else's.** If three contractors quote $12,000-$14,000 and one quotes $7,000, that last one isn't giving you a deal — they're leaving something out.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many estimates should I get before hiring a roofer?**
Three is the standard recommendation. Getting at least three written estimates gives you a realistic picture of what the job should cost and helps you spot outliers — both high and low. Focus less on finding the cheapest price and more on finding the best value: quality materials, solid warranties, and a contractor you trust.

**Should I hire a roofer who comes to my door after a storm?**
Proceed with extreme caution. While some door-knockers are legitimate local companies, many are storm chasers — out-of-state crews who follow severe weather to collect insurance payouts. They often do subpar work and are impossible to reach when issues arise. If someone knocks on your door, run them through every question on this list before even considering a contract.

**What should a roofing contract include?**
At minimum: a detailed scope of work, material specifications (brand, product line, color), total cost with line-item breakdown, payment schedule, start and completion dates, warranty terms (both manufacturer and workmanship), a process for change orders, and proof of insurance and licensing. If any of these are missing, ask for them before you sign.

**Is the cheapest roofing estimate the best choice?**
Almost never. The lowest bid usually means cheaper materials, skipped steps (like not replacing underlayment or flashing), or no workmanship warranty. A roof is a 25-30 year investment. Saving $2,000 today on a contractor who cuts corners will cost you far more in repairs and early replacement down the road.
