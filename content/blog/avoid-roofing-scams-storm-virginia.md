# How to Avoid Roofing Scams After a Storm in Virginia

After a big storm rolls through Southwest Virginia, the damage is obvious — missing shingles, dented gutters, tree limbs through the deck. What's less obvious is the wave of strangers about to show up at your door.

Within 24 to 48 hours of any major hail or wind event in the Roanoke or New River Valley area, out-of-state trucks start appearing in neighborhoods. Guys with clipboards knock on doors. They're polite. They're confident. They'll point at your roof and tell you they can see damage from the street. And before you know it, they're asking you to sign something.

These are storm chasers — roofing crews that follow severe weather across multiple states, collect as many contracts as they can, do fast and cheap work (or sometimes no work at all), and move on to the next disaster. They have no stake in your community, no local reputation to protect, and no intention of being around when problems show up six months later.

This post is here to help you tell the difference between a legitimate Virginia roofing contractor and someone looking to take advantage of a stressful situation.

## 8 Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

### 1. They Knock on Your Door Uninvited

A stranger showing up the day after a storm offering a "free roof inspection" is the single most common starting point for roofing scams after a storm in Virginia. Legitimate contractors stay busy through referrals, repeat customers, and their existing reputation. They don't need to canvass neighborhoods.

That doesn't mean every door-knocker is a scammer. But it means your guard should be up immediately.

### 2. "Don't Worry — Insurance Will Cover Everything"

Any roofer who guarantees your insurance will pay for a full replacement before an adjuster has even looked at your roof is lying to you. They don't control what your insurance company approves. What they're really doing is trying to get you to sign a contract based on a promise they can't keep.

Some will even offer to "handle everything with your insurance" and inflate the claim. That's insurance fraud, and you — the homeowner — are the one who ends up liable.

### 3. No Local Address

Ask where their office is. If the answer is vague ("we're based out of Roanoke" with no actual street address) or they hand you a card with only a phone number and a Gmail address, that's a problem. A contractor who has been doing business in the New River Valley for years will have a physical presence you can verify.

### 4. They Can't Provide a Virginia License Number

Virginia requires contractors to hold a valid license issued by the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR). More on this below, but the short version: if they can't give you a license number on the spot, stop the conversation.

### 5. They Want Cash Up Front or "A Deposit to Hold Materials"

Asking for full payment before work starts is a textbook roofing scam warning sign. A reasonable deposit for a large job is normal — but it should be clearly outlined in a written contract, and you should never pay cash without a receipt. Storm chasers love cash because it leaves no trail.

### 6. They Pressure You to Sign Right Now

"This price is only good today." "We've got crews in the area this week, but after that we're booked." "If you wait, your damage will get worse and insurance won't cover it."

All of these are pressure tactics. A trustworthy roofer will give you a written estimate and let you take your time. They know their work speaks for itself.

### 7. Their Bid Is Way Lower Than Everyone Else's

If you get three quotes and one comes in 40% below the others, that's not a deal — that's a warning. Lowball bids usually mean they're cutting corners on materials, skipping proper underlayment, using subcontractors they've never worked with, or planning to hit you with change orders once the job starts.

### 8. Temporary Signage and No Online Presence

Magnetic truck signs. A website that was registered last month. No Google reviews. No BBB listing. No photos of past projects. These are signs of a company that was set up quickly, designed to look legitimate for a few weeks, and will vanish once the storm season is over.

## What Virginia Law Actually Requires

Virginia takes contractor licensing seriously, and knowing the rules gives you a real advantage when you're trying to spot a roofing scam.

**Contractor Licensing:**
- Any roofing job over $1,000 requires the contractor to hold a valid **Class A, B, or C license** from the Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR)
- Class A covers jobs over $120,000; Class B covers $10,000 to $120,000; Class C covers $1,000 to $10,000
- The license must be in the contractor's legal business name

**Insurance Requirements:**
- Licensed contractors in Virginia must carry **general liability insurance** and **workers' compensation insurance** (if they have employees)
- If a worker is injured on your property and the contractor doesn't have workers' comp, you could be held responsible

**Contract Requirements:**
- Virginia's Home Solicitation Sales Act gives you a **three-day right to cancel** any contract signed at your home
- A legitimate contractor will never pressure you to waive that right

## How to Verify a Roofing Contractor Before You Sign Anything

You don't have to take anyone's word for it. Here's how to check for yourself:

**1. Look Them Up on DPOR**
Go to [dpor.virginia.gov](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov) and search their license number or business name. You'll see their license class, status, and whether any complaints have been filed. This takes about two minutes and it's the single best thing you can do.

**2. Check the BBB**
The Better Business Bureau isn't perfect, but a pattern of unresolved complaints is a clear signal. Also look at how the company responds to complaints — that tells you a lot about how they operate.

**3. Read Google Reviews (Carefully)**
Look for reviews that mention specific projects, specific crew members, or specific neighborhoods in the Christiansburg, Blacksburg, Radford, or Roanoke areas. Generic five-star reviews that all appeared in the same week are often fake.

**4. Ask for References and Go See Their Work**
A contractor who has been working in Southwest Virginia for years should be able to point you to multiple homes in the area where you can see their roofing firsthand. If they can't, ask yourself why.

**5. Verify Manufacturer Certifications**
Certifications like **GAF Master Elite** are earned, not bought. Only about 3% of roofing contractors in the country hold that designation. It means the manufacturer has vetted the contractor's installation practices, business stability, and customer satisfaction record. Ask for proof and verify it on the manufacturer's website.

## Why Storm Chasers Target Virginia

Virginia's location makes it a prime target. We get severe thunderstorms, hail, high winds from tropical systems, and the occasional ice storm — all of which cause legitimate roof damage. Storm chaser roofers monitor weather radar across multiple states and deploy crews to wherever the damage is worst.

They're not based here. They don't hire locally. They often use untrained labor, skip permits, and use the cheapest materials available. When your roof starts leaking again in eight months, the phone number on that business card won't connect to anyone.

That's the real cost of a storm chaser — not just the money you lose up front, but the second repair bill when you have to hire a real contractor to fix what they did wrong.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I report a roofing scam in Virginia?
File a complaint with the Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR) at dpor.virginia.gov. You can also report fraud to the Virginia Attorney General's Consumer Protection Section. If you signed a contract under pressure, remember that Virginia law gives you three business days to cancel a home solicitation contract.

### Can I do my own roof repair after storm damage instead of hiring a contractor?
You can make temporary repairs to prevent further damage — tarping exposed areas, for example. But permanent roof repair or replacement should be done by a licensed contractor. Insurance claims typically require professional documentation and workmanship, and DIY repairs can void your policy coverage or your roof's manufacturer warranty.

### What should I do first after storm damage to my roof?
Document the damage with photos and video before anything gets moved or cleaned up. Contact your insurance company to start a claim. Then get written estimates from two or three licensed, local roofing contractors. Don't let anyone start work until your insurance adjuster has inspected the damage, and don't sign anything the same day someone shows up at your door.

### How much should a legitimate roof repair cost after a storm?
It depends entirely on the extent of the damage — a few missing shingles is a very different job than replacing an entire roof slope. In the New River Valley and Roanoke areas, a full shingle replacement on an average-sized home typically runs between $8,000 and $15,000 depending on materials and complexity. Be suspicious of any quote that's dramatically lower than others you've received.
