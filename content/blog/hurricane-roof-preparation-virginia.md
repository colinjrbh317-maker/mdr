# How to Prepare Your Roof for Hurricane Season in Virginia

**Meta Description:** Learn how to prepare your roof for hurricane season in Virginia. A step-by-step checklist covering inspections, repairs, and storm-readiness tips from a GAF Master Elite contractor.

**Target Keyword:** hurricane roof preparation virginia

---

Most Virginia homeowners don't think of themselves as living in hurricane country. But every year from June through November, the remnants of tropical systems push inland from the coast and hit Southwest Virginia with sustained heavy rain, high winds, and the occasional downpour that dumps several inches in a matter of hours. The Blue Ridge and New River Valley may sit a few hundred miles from the coast, but those storm remnants still pack enough punch to tear off shingles, flood gutters, and send tree limbs through roof decking.

The good news: most hurricane-related roof damage is preventable. If you spend a few hours on preparation before storm season arrives, your roof will handle whatever rolls in off the Atlantic far better than the one next door that hasn't been touched since installation.

Here's exactly what to do, step by step.

## Your Pre-Storm Roof Preparation Checklist

### 1. Inspect Your Shingles for Damage or Wear

Walk your property and look up. You're checking for curling edges, cracked shingles, missing tabs, and dark spots where granules have worn away. These weak points are exactly where wind gets underneath and starts peeling material off during a storm.

If you have binoculars, use them. If you're not comfortable on a ladder, call a roofer — most reputable companies will do a visual inspection at no charge.

Pay extra attention to the south- and west-facing slopes. In our part of Virginia, those faces take the most weather year-round and tend to show wear first.

### 2. Check All Flashing and Sealant Points

Flashing is the metal strips around chimneys, vents, skylights, and where roof planes meet walls. When flashing lifts, cracks, or separates, water finds a direct path into the structure beneath. During a tropical rain event that drops two or three inches in an hour, even a small gap in flashing can let in enough water to damage ceilings and insulation.

Look for rust, gaps, and sealant that has dried and cracked. Roof caulk and sealant have a limited lifespan and need to be reapplied periodically.

### 3. Clean and Secure Your Gutters

Clogged gutters are one of the top causes of water damage during heavy rain. When gutters back up, water pools along the roof edge, works under the drip edge, and rots fascia boards and soffit.

Clear all leaves and debris, flush downspouts with a hose, and make sure every gutter section is firmly fastened. Check that downspout extensions direct water at least four to six feet away from the foundation. In the New River Valley, the clay-heavy soil doesn't drain fast, so standing water near the foundation during a heavy storm can turn into a basement problem quickly.

### 4. Trim Back Overhanging Trees

This is the single most impactful thing many homeowners can do before storm season. Branches that hang over or near the roof are the number one source of storm damage in Southwest Virginia — not from wind alone, but from wind plus a heavy limb swinging into your ridge cap or puncturing a roof plane.

Cut back any branch within six to eight feet of the roof surface. Dead branches anywhere in the tree should come down regardless of distance. If a mature hardwood leans toward the house and you've been putting off dealing with it, storm season is your deadline.

### 5. Document Your Roof's Current Condition

Before a single cloud forms, take dated photos of every side of your roof and any existing wear points. Save them somewhere you won't lose them — email them to yourself, put them in a cloud folder, whatever works.

If a storm does cause damage, these before photos make the insurance claims process dramatically faster. Without them, adjusters have to guess what was pre-existing versus storm-caused, and that guessing usually doesn't go in your favor.

### 6. Review Your Insurance Policy

Pull out your homeowner's policy and read the roofing section specifically. Know your deductible for wind and hail damage (they're sometimes different from your standard deductible). Know whether your policy covers full replacement cost or only depreciated value — the difference can be tens of thousands of dollars on a full roof replacement.

If anything is unclear, call your agent now, not after a storm when they're fielding hundreds of calls.

### 7. Secure Loose Items Around the Exterior

Patio furniture, potted plants, grills, kids' toys, and anything else sitting on your deck or in the yard becomes a projectile in 60+ mph wind gusts. Store what you can in a garage or shed. What you can't store, anchor down.

This protects your roof and your neighbor's roof. A plastic Adirondack chair launched by a gust can crack vinyl siding or break a window just as easily as a tree limb.

### 8. Know Your Emergency Plan

Have a plan for what happens if your roof takes a hit during a storm. Know where your tarps are. Know the number of a roofing contractor you trust — not one you'll have to Google in a panic at 11 p.m. during a power outage. Know where your water shutoff is in case a roof breach floods the interior.

Write this down. Tape it inside a kitchen cabinet. When things go sideways, you won't be thinking clearly, and having a written plan takes the guesswork out.

## How Roofing Materials Hold Up to Hurricane-Force Winds

Not all roofing materials perform equally when the wind picks up. If you're considering a roof replacement before storm season, here's what the numbers look like:

- **GAF Timberline HDZ Shingles** — Rated for winds up to 130 mph with the LayerLock nailing zone. This is what we install most often in the Christiansburg and Blacksburg area. When installed to GAF specifications by a certified contractor, these shingles carry one of the strongest wind warranties in the asphalt shingle category.
- **Standing Seam Metal Roofing** — Rated for winds up to 160 mph depending on panel profile and gauge. Metal roofs also shed water faster than shingles, which matters during the sustained heavy rain that tropical remnants bring to the Blue Ridge region.
- **3-Tab Shingles** — Most are rated for only 60-70 mph winds. If your home still has 3-tab shingles and they're more than 15 years old, they're the most likely to fail during a storm.

Material matters, but installation matters just as much. A 130 mph-rated shingle installed with the wrong nailing pattern or inadequate starter strip drops to maybe half that effective rating. This is one of the reasons manufacturer certifications like GAF Master Elite exist — they ensure the installation meets the standard the warranty is based on.

## What to Do During and Immediately After a Storm

**During the storm:** Stay inside. No roof issue is worth your safety. If you hear something hit the roof or notice a leak starting, place buckets to catch water and move furniture and electronics away from the affected area. That's all you can do until the storm passes.

**Within the first 24 hours after the storm:**

1. Walk the perimeter of your home and look for obvious damage — missing shingles, downed limbs on the roof, dented gutters, displaced flashing.
2. Check your attic for signs of water intrusion. Look for wet insulation, water stains on the underside of the decking, and daylight showing through where it shouldn't.
3. Take photos of everything before touching anything. Document first, clean up second.
4. If there's an active leak or structural damage, call a contractor immediately for emergency tarping. Do not attempt to tarp a roof yourself in wet or windy conditions.
5. File your insurance claim early. After a major storm event, adjusters get backed up for weeks. The earlier you file, the earlier you're in the queue.

**A note on storm chasers:** After any significant weather event, out-of-state roofing crews will canvass neighborhoods door-to-door. Many are legitimate, but many are not. Before signing anything, verify the company holds a Virginia contractor's license, carries insurance, and has a physical business address. A local contractor who'll be here next year is always a safer bet than a crew that followed the storm in from three states away.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When is hurricane season in Virginia?

Atlantic hurricane season runs from June 1 through November 30. In Southwest Virginia, the highest risk for tropical remnants hitting the area is typically August through October, when storms that make landfall along the Gulf or Southeast coast track inland toward the Appalachian region.

### How often should I have my roof inspected before storm season?

At minimum, once per year — ideally in late spring before storm season begins. If your roof is more than 15 years old or you noticed any issues during the winter, a second inspection in early fall is a good idea. Most qualified roofing contractors offer free inspections.

### Will my homeowner's insurance cover hurricane damage to my roof?

Most standard policies cover wind damage, but the specifics vary. Some policies have separate, higher deductibles for named storms or hurricanes. Some pay replacement cost while others pay only actual cash value (depreciated). Read your policy now and talk to your agent before you need to file a claim.

### Can I prepare my roof for a storm myself, or do I need a professional?

You can handle several items on this checklist yourself — cleaning gutters, trimming small branches, securing loose items, and documenting your roof's condition. But for anything that requires getting on the roof, evaluating structural issues, or making repairs to flashing and shingles, call a professional. One slip on a wet roof isn't worth saving a service call fee.
